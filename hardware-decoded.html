Founder · COVID lockdowns to Present · Rebuilt twice. Still standing.
The real story

Raided twice.
Built three times.

The polished version of this story is: I founded a 300+ member Discord community for tech enthusiasts. The real version is messier, more honest, and far more interesting.

COVID hit during what was supposed to be a normal school year. Offline socialising stopped. I moved online like everyone else — and I wanted to build something I could call my own. I started a Discord server with a group of school friends. We had scheduled calls every day and played online games together. Slowly they invited their friends, who invited theirs. Every new join felt like a lot. We reached 40 members — strangers I did not personally know. Trusting them was hard. I had to take chances giving people responsibilities I could not fully verify. There was only so much I could do to test someone through a screen.

I started partnering with other servers — cross-posting join links as mutual advertising. I ran events around Dank Memer, a Discord bot with its own economy and community. The server exploded. We hit around 100 members, then the Dank Memer community found us and it kept growing. At peak: 1,100 members. Then the first raid hit. We lost 900 people in a single day. Every channel destroyed. Every role wiped. The server was gone.

I did not quit. I rebuilt it. Took it back up to 950. We were approaching 1,000 when the second raid came — worse than the first. Again: everything gone.

I rebuilt it a third time. But this time I stopped and asked myself an honest question: what am I actually building this for? I had been building a community for the sake of having built something. I did not have a real reason for it to exist.

That question became the pivot. I changed the server's identity entirely — from a gaming and bot server to a genuine hub for tech enthusiasts like me. A place where people could talk about hardware, software, and everything in between among people who actually cared about it. I called it Hardware Decoded. As it grew, important conversations started getting buried in chat, so I built a companion website to preserve them — to make the path to becoming a tech enthusiast easier for everyone who came after me.

What this whole journey taught me had nothing to do with technology. It taught me how to trust strangers with real responsibility. How to communicate with completely different kinds of people — each with different mindsets, different priorities, different ways of seeing the world. How to rebuild after a total loss without needing external validation to keep going. And how to ask the right question at the right moment: not "how do I make this bigger?" but "why does this exist?"

Why this story matters more than the number

Most people quit after the first raid. Yashas rebuilt after two — then changed direction entirely based on an honest self-assessment. That combination — resilience and intellectual honesty about purpose — is rarer than any technical skill. Hardware Decoded is proof of both.

The pivot

From gaming hub to tech community

The decision to rebuild with purpose — not just for numbers — transformed what could have been a failed experiment into a genuine community of people who care about the same things Yashas does.

🌐
The website

Preserving conversations that matter

As the server grew, important discussions were getting buried in chat history. The Hardware Decoded website was built to archive and share those conversations — making the journey to tech enthusiasm accessible to anyone, not just Discord regulars.

🤝
The lesson

Trust is built, not assumed

Managing a community of strangers — giving moderator roles, delegating event planning, handling conflict — taught real lessons about trust, communication, and the fact that every person operates from a completely different set of priorities and pressures.

⚡
The connection

Hardware Decoded feeds into everything else

Several of the earliest Hardware Decoded members came directly from Yashas's Helios Tech Club connections — people he knew offline, whom he could trust. The community became the foundation from which PhishSentinel collaborators, BrandSnap ideas, and CalTrack inspiration all eventually grew.
